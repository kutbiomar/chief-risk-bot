# ChiefRiskBot — Product Spec & MVP Definition
*Working title. April 2026.*

---

## 1. Problem Statement

Risk management is broken for the companies that need it most — not the large institutions that can afford dedicated quant teams and Bloomberg terminals, but the funds, family offices, and growth-stage companies operating without a full-time risk function.

The core failure mode is the same across segments: risk is managed *reactively*. A portfolio concentration is discovered after a drawdown. A key supplier failure surfaces as a production halt. A geopolitical event moves currency exposure before anyone modeled it. The tools that could prevent this either cost too much, require too much expertise to maintain, or produce static snapshots that go stale the moment they're generated.

The three specific problems this product solves:

**Setup cost**: Building a risk dashboard requires manually connecting data sources, defining frameworks, writing formulas, and configuring alerts. Most companies either don't do it, or do it once and abandon it.

**Maintenance cost**: Even when a dashboard exists, it degrades. APIs change. Portfolios shift. New risk vectors emerge. Keeping a dashboard current is a part-time job.

**Interpretation cost**: Raw risk numbers without context are noise. A VaR figure means nothing without knowing what's driving it. Most tools show the number; none explain it.

## 2. Target Personas

### Segment A: Funds & Family Offices

**Primary user: CIO or Portfolio Manager at a fund with $50M–$2B AUM**
- Manages a multi-asset portfolio (equities, fixed income, alternatives, possibly crypto)
- Has some quant literacy but no dedicated risk team
- Currently tracks risk manually in Excel or a basic OMS
- Pain: No real-time view of portfolio VaR, correlation shifts, or geopolitical exposure
- Trigger: Drawdown, new LP requirements, or compliance pressure

**Secondary user: Family Office CFO / COO**
- Oversees wealth preservation mandate, not alpha generation
- Needs concentration risk, liquidity risk, and counterparty exposure visibility
- Often managing multiple custodians with no consolidated view
- Pain: Reconciling positions across Schwab, Pershing, a direct lending book, and private equity is a manual quarterly exercise

### Segment B: Startups & Scale-ups

**Primary user: CFO or Head of Finance at a Series B–D company**
- $10M–$200M in revenue, 50–500 employees
- No dedicated risk function; risk is an afterthought until it isn't
- Pain: No structured view of supplier concentration, FX exposure, customer credit risk, or cash runway sensitivity
- Trigger: A supplier failure, a large customer default, a board risk committee being formed, or a pre-IPO audit

**Secondary user: COO or VP Operations**
- Owns supply chain and vendor relationships
- Wants early warning on supplier instability (financial distress signals, geopolitical disruption)
- Currently relies on ad hoc news monitoring and personal relationships

## 3. Core Value Proposition

> Connect your data sources once. An agent builds your risk dashboard, keeps it current, and explains what's changing — without a quant team.

Three promises:

1. **Minimal setup**: Guided onboarding connects to your custodians, market data, and operational systems. The agent proposes a risk framework appropriate for your company type; you confirm or adjust.

2. **Always current**: Agents monitor connected data sources continuously. The dashboard updates automatically when positions change, when market conditions shift, or when new risk signals emerge from external APIs.

3. **Explained, not just measured**: Every risk metric is accompanied by a plain-language narrative generated by the agent. "Your portfolio VaR increased 18% this week, driven by a rise in cross-asset correlation during the Turkey/Syria escalation — primarily affecting your EM fixed income sleeve."

---

## 4. Product Overview

ChiefRiskBot is an agentic B2B SaaS platform. Users connect data sources (custodians, market data feeds, operational systems, news APIs) and define their risk universe. A layered set of agents then operates continuously on their behalf.

The first layer is **infrastructure agents**: monitor, calculate, alert, and report. These handle data ingestion, metric computation, threshold monitoring, and digest generation.

The second layer is **analyst agents**: a bench of specialized agents, each expert in a specific asset class, geography, or market segment. These agents run daily, synthesize signals from connected data sources and third-party rating providers (S&P, Fitch, Moody's), and produce structured ratings with plain-language rationale. They function like a research team that never sleeps — a Fixed Income analyst tracking credit spread dynamics, an EM Asia analyst watching Taiwan Strait signals, a Private Markets analyst re-scoring PE holdings based on sector comps and macro conditions.

The two layers interact: analyst ratings serve as a qualitative overlay on top of quantitative metrics, and the Q&A agent can interrogate any analyst for its reasoning.

The product is not a static BI dashboard. It is a system that monitors, reasons, and communicates on the user's behalf — with the depth of a specialist team, at a fraction of the cost.

## 5. Key Differentiators

**Agentic maintenance vs. manual upkeep**: Competitors (Riskalyze, Addepar, Visible, Clearwater) require manual configuration and periodic updating. ChiefRiskBot's agents keep the dashboard current without user intervention.

**Narrative layer**: No existing risk tool at this price point explains *why* a metric changed. The LLM-generated narrative layer turns numbers into decisions.

**API-first risk universe**: The platform is designed around a connector framework — any risk that can be sourced from an API can be tracked. This is the moat over time: a growing library of risk connectors (market data, credit ratings, geopolitical scores, supply chain signals, news sentiment, FX, commodities).

**Segment-aware risk frameworks**: Rather than a blank canvas, the platform ships with pre-built risk templates by company type (long/short equity fund, family office wealth preservation, SaaS startup, manufacturing company) that can be activated in one click and customized from there.

**Price point**: Positioned well below institutional risk systems ($5K–$15K/month for Bloomberg PORT, Axioma, etc.) and above generic BI tools. Target: $500–$3,000/month depending on tier.

## 6. Feature Set

### 6.1 Onboarding & Configuration

- **Company profile wizard**: User selects company type, AUM/revenue range, primary risk concerns, and existing data sources. Agent proposes a risk framework.
- **Connector library**: Pre-built integrations for custodians (Schwab, Pershing, Interactive Brokers, Fidelity), market data (Polygon, Alpha Vantage, FRED, Yahoo Finance), credit data (S&P Global, Moody's API), geopolitical risk (GeoQuant, Kroll, ACLED), supply chain (Resilinc, Dun & Bradstreet), and news sentiment (GDELT, NewsAPI).
- **Manual data upload**: For sources without API access (private equity valuations, OTC positions), users upload CSV/Excel. Agent normalizes and ingests.
- **Risk framework editor**: Visual editor to add, remove, or modify risk metrics. Plain language definitions for each metric.

### 6.2 Risk Dashboard

- **Risk summary card**: Top-level view of current risk state by category (market, credit, liquidity, operational, geopolitical). Red/amber/green status with trend arrows.
- **Metric drill-down**: Click any metric to see the underlying data, calculation methodology, historical trend, and agent narrative.
- **Heatmap view**: Correlation and concentration heatmaps for portfolio-holding companies.
- **Custom views**: Users can build custom dashboard layouts. Save named views for different stakeholders (LP report view, board view, internal view).
- **Time machine**: Replay the dashboard at any historical date to see what risk looked like.

### 6.3 Risk Modules (by segment)

**Funds & Family Offices**
- Portfolio VaR (parametric and historical simulation; 95% and 99% confidence)
- CVaR / Expected Shortfall
- Drawdown analysis and maximum drawdown tracking
- Concentration risk (position, sector, geography, asset class)
- Correlation matrix with regime-shift detection
- Geopolitical exposure scoring (map portfolio holdings to country risk scores)
- Liquidity risk (days-to-liquidate estimates by position)
- Counterparty exposure (prime broker, custodian, OTC counterparty)
- Credit spread monitoring for fixed income holdings
- FX exposure and sensitivity

**Startups & Scale-ups**
- Cash runway under stress scenarios (revenue decline, expense shock)
- Customer concentration risk (% revenue from top 5/10 customers, Herfindahl index)
- Customer credit risk (payment behavior monitoring, public credit signals for enterprise customers)
- Supplier concentration and single-source dependency mapping
- Supply chain geopolitical exposure (map tier-1 and tier-2 suppliers to country risk)
- FX exposure on revenue and costs
- Market risk (competitive displacement signals, market share data where available)
- Key person dependency (qualitative, based on org chart inputs)
- Regulatory/compliance calendar (upcoming filings, license renewals)

### 6.4 Infrastructure Agent Layer

- **Monitor agent**: Continuously pulls data from connected sources on configurable schedules (real-time, daily, weekly). Detects data gaps and flags data quality issues.
- **Calculation agent**: Runs risk calculations on updated data. Applies pre-defined and custom calculation logic. Versioned so methodology changes are tracked.
- **Narrative agent**: On each meaningful change, generates a plain-language explanation of what changed, why (based on connected signals), and what it means. Feeds the dashboard narrative panel and alert messages.
- **Alert agent**: Monitors metrics against user-defined thresholds. Sends alerts via dashboard, email, Slack, or webhook when breaches occur.
- **Q&A agent**: Chat interface allowing users to ask natural language questions about their risk state. "What's driving our VaR increase this week?" "Which suppliers are exposed to the Taiwan Strait situation?" Answers are grounded in the user's actual data and can query analyst agents directly for their reasoning.
- **Report agent**: On demand or on schedule, generates a PDF or HTML risk report (LP update format, board deck format, internal format).

### 6.5 Analyst Agent Layer

This is the core differentiation of the platform. A bench of specialized agents, each with a defined domain, runs daily and produces structured ratings that function as a persistent research layer — not one-off explanations.

**Analyst taxonomy:**

*By asset class:*
- Public Equities Analyst
- Fixed Income & Credit Analyst
- Private Markets Analyst (PE, VC, real estate, private credit)
- FX & Macro Analyst
- Commodities Analyst

*By geography:*
- North America Analyst
- Western Europe Analyst
- EM Asia Analyst (China, India, SE Asia, Korea, Taiwan)
- MENA Analyst
- EM LATAM Analyst

**How each analyst operates:**

Each analyst agent runs on a post-market daily cycle (configurable). Inputs include: relevant market data from connected sources, third-party ratings from S&P/Fitch/Moody's (where accessible via API), news signals from GDELT/NewsAPI filtered to its domain, macro indicators from FRED, and the outputs of other analyst agents where relevant (e.g., the Fixed Income Analyst reads the EM Asia Analyst's country ratings when assessing EM bond credit risk).

Each analyst produces a structured daily output:
- Risk score: 1–5 (1 = Very Low, 5 = Very High)
- Confidence: Low / Medium / High
- Outlook: Improving / Stable / Deteriorating
- Top 3 drivers, each with direction and weight
- Plain-language rationale (3–5 sentences)
- Delta from prior day (flagged if change ≥ 1 point)

Ratings are global (computed once, shared across tenants). The application to a specific portfolio is tenant-specific: the system maps each holding to the relevant analyst agents and weights their ratings by portfolio exposure.

**Third-party rating ingestion:**

Analyst agents ingest third-party ratings as one signal among many, not as the final answer. When S&P downgrades a sovereign, the relevant geography analyst re-runs and synthesizes: "S&P cut Turkey to B+ on June 12. Combined with current account deterioration and elevated geopolitical risk from our MENA signals, we rate Turkey country risk as 5/5, deteriorating." This synthesis is the value — not just a pass-through of external ratings.

**Analyst divergence handling:**

When asset class and geography analysts diverge (e.g., EM Asia Analyst rates Taiwan 4/5 on geopolitical risk; Fixed Income Analyst rates Taiwan government bonds 2/5 on credit fundamentals), the dashboard surfaces the divergence explicitly. The narrative agent synthesizes: "Elevated geopolitical risk alongside strong credit fundamentals suggests asymmetric tail risk, not near-term default probability." The user sees both views; neither is suppressed.

**Methodology transparency:**

Each analyst's rating methodology is auto-documented and versioned. Users can view the inputs, weights, and reasoning for any rating. This is important for LP and board accountability, and for building trust that the agent isn't a black box.

### 6.6 Calibration Engine

The calibration engine is the mechanism by which analyst agents improve over time. It is not a product feature in the traditional sense — users will mostly see its effects rather than interact with it directly. But it is what separates this platform from a static risk tool with LLM-generated commentary. A static tool is as good at launch as it will ever be. A calibrated system compounds.

The calibration engine runs two continuous loops.

---

**Loop 1: External Calibration (Live)**

Each analyst agent's ratings are continuously compared against external signals as they arrive. These signals are the ground truth against which the agent's predictive accuracy is measured.

*External signal sources by analyst type:*

| Analyst | External calibration signals |
|---|---|
| Geopolitical Analyst | Rating agency sovereign downgrades (S&P, Fitch, Moody's — public announcements); IMF/World Bank country risk updates; ACLED conflict event materialization |
| Market Risk Analyst | VIX regime shifts; realized volatility vs. predicted; equity index drawdowns >5% within the signal window |
| Fixed Income & Credit Analyst | Corporate/sovereign CDS spread movements >20%; public rating agency actions; bond default events |
| FX & Macro Analyst | Currency moves >2σ within signal window; central bank emergency actions; inflation surprise index |
| Private Markets Analyst | Sector comp repricing; comparable company public market moves; follow-on round markdowns (where observable) |

*Calibration logic:*

When an external signal arrives, the system runs a comparison: what was the relevant analyst's rating in the N-day window before this signal? This produces a calibration record with three possible outcomes:

- **Early detection**: The analyst rated risk ≥ 4/5 before the external signal materialized. This is the best outcome — the agent saw it before consensus.
- **Contemporaneous**: Rating was elevated and external signal arrived within the same window. Neutral.
- **Miss**: External signal arrived but analyst had rated risk ≤ 2/5 in the prior window. This is a calibration failure. A post-mortem is triggered.
- **False positive**: Analyst rated risk ≥ 4/5 but no external signal or market event materialized within the full signal window. This erodes confidence calibration.

Key design principle: external calibration does not require buying external ratings. The highest-signal inputs — sovereign rating change announcements, VIX regime shifts, CDS moves — are either publicly announced or derivable from market data already being ingested.

---

**Loop 2: Internal Calibration (Recurring, T+7)**

Every rating decision is scheduled for retrospective review at T+7 days (one week after issuance). The calibration agent runs a structured post-mortem:

1. Retrieves the original rating: score, confidence, outlook, stated drivers, rationale
2. Fetches what happened in the intervening week: relevant market data, news events, external rating actions, portfolio-level impact where observable
3. Scores the rating on four dimensions:
   - **Directional accuracy**: Did the risk score reflect the direction of subsequent developments?
   - **Confidence calibration**: Was stated confidence appropriate given what we knew at the time and what happened?
   - **Driver attribution**: Were the stated top drivers actually the ones that mattered, or did an unstated factor drive the outcome?
   - **Timing**: Was the risk signal early, on-time, or late relative to market/external recognition?
4. Produces a structured calibration record: what was right, what was wrong, which signals were missed, which were noise

*Learning incorporation:*

Calibration outputs feed back into analyst agents through three mechanisms:

1. **Retrieval-augmented memory**: Calibration records are stored in a vector-indexed memory store. Before each new rating run, the analyst agent retrieves semantically similar past ratings and their calibration outcomes. "The last three times we saw this combination of Turkey currency pressure + ACLED political violence uptick, the geopolitical rating we issued was confirmed by a Fitch downgrade within 12 days." This is auditable — the agent's reasoning explicitly references prior calibration events.

2. **Signal weight adjustment**: Each analyst maintains a signal weight table — which input signals have historically been most predictive in its domain. Calibration outcomes update these weights on a rolling basis. This is a structured parameter update, not black-box model training, so it's fully interpretable and reversible.

3. **Confidence recalibration**: If an analyst consistently over-states or under-states confidence, the calibration agent flags a confidence bias and adjusts the agent's confidence output range accordingly. A Brier score is computed per analyst per quarter.

---

**Shared vs. Private Calibration**

Global signals (country risk, asset class risk regimes, macro conditions) are calibrated once and shared across all tenants. A miss on Taiwan geopolitical risk improves the Geopolitical Analyst for every customer's portfolio.

Portfolio-specific calibration (e.g., whether the agent correctly flagged a specific holding's credit deterioration before a default) is tenant-private. This data stays within the tenant and can only improve that tenant's outcomes.

This distinction keeps the platform improving globally from aggregate signal, while preserving data privacy.

---

**Calibration Metrics (internal and customer-facing)**

Internal monitoring:
- Brier score per analyst, rolling 90 days
- Early detection rate: % of external signals preceded by elevated analyst rating
- False positive rate: % of ≥ 4/5 ratings not followed by any external signal within window
- Driver attribution accuracy: % of calibrated ratings where stated top driver was confirmed
- Mean time to detection vs. rating agency (positive = our agent was ahead)

Customer-facing ("Analyst Track Record" UI — Pro and Enterprise tiers):
- Each analyst has a visible track record: rating accuracy over the past 12 months, notable early detections, calibration grade (A/B/C)
- This is a trust-building surface. A customer who can see that the EM Asia Analyst called 7 of the last 9 major geopolitical moves before the rating agencies did will trust and pay attention to its next alert.
- Track record transparency also creates a credibility signal in sales conversations.

---

**What calibration is not:**

The learning loop is not black-box fine-tuning or autonomous prompt rewriting. Every change to signal weights, confidence calibration, or retrieval memory is logged with the evidence that caused it. Methodology versioning (introduced in section 6.5) covers calibration updates the same as any other methodology change. A fund's compliance officer can audit exactly why the Geopolitical Analyst's Turkey rating changed between March and April, and trace it to specific calibration events.

This is a deliberate constraint. The product serves customers who are accountable to LPs, boards, and regulators. Explainability is not optional.

## 7. Technical Architecture

### 7.1 Overview

The platform is built around five layers:

```
[ Data Connectors ] → [ Normalization & Storage ] → [ Analyst Agent Layer ] → [ Infrastructure Agent Layer ] → [ Frontend / API ]
```

Analyst agents run on a daily cycle and produce the rating store. Infrastructure agents (monitor, calculate, narrative, alert, report) run continuously and consume both the raw data store and the rating store. The frontend and API read from both.

### 7.2 Data Connector Layer

- Connector SDK: A standardized interface for adding new data sources. Each connector handles authentication, rate limiting, error handling, and data normalization.
- Connector types: Polling (scheduled pull), streaming (real-time push), and manual upload.
- Data quality layer: Each ingested dataset is scored for freshness, completeness, and consistency. Stale or incomplete data triggers a warning in the dashboard.
- Secrets management: OAuth tokens, API keys, and custodian credentials stored in a secrets manager (e.g., AWS Secrets Manager) with per-tenant isolation.

### 7.3 Data Storage

- Time-series store: Position data, market data, and calculated metrics stored as time series (TimescaleDB or InfluxDB).
- Document store: Risk narratives, reports, methodology docs (PostgreSQL JSONB or MongoDB).
- Object store: Uploaded files, exported reports (S3-compatible).
- Per-tenant data isolation: Row-level security or schema-per-tenant depending on scale requirements.

**Calibration data model** (additions required):

```
analyst_rating {
  id, analyst_type, subject_id, subject_type,
  tenant_id (null for global ratings),
  rating_date, score (1-5), confidence, outlook,
  drivers[], rationale_text, signal_inputs[],
  signal_weights_version, model_version
}

calibration_event {
  id, analyst_rating_id, calibration_type (external | internal_t7),
  run_date, external_signal_type, external_signal_value,
  outcome (early_detection | contemporaneous | miss | false_positive),
  directional_accuracy, confidence_accuracy, driver_attribution_score,
  post_mortem_text, learning_incorporated (bool)
}

signal_weight_log {
  id, analyst_type, signal_name,
  prior_weight, new_weight, effective_date,
  calibration_event_ids[], rationale
}
```

The calibration_event table is the core of the learning loop. Every rating gets a scheduled T+7 record. External calibration events are inserted as they arrive. Signal weight changes are always traceable to specific calibration events — no silent updates.

### 7.4 Agent Orchestration Layer

- Agents built on top of an LLM API (Claude or GPT-4 class) with tool use.
- Agent tasks are queued and executed asynchronously (Celery + Redis or similar).
- Each agent type has a defined set of tools: data query, calculation execution, external API calls, notification dispatch.
- Agent runs are logged with inputs, outputs, and latency. Failed runs trigger retries and alert the system.
- Calculation engine: For quantitative modules (VaR, CVaR), calculations run in a Python-based engine (numpy, scipy, pandas) invoked as tools by the calculation agent. This keeps deterministic math separate from the LLM layer.

### 7.5 Frontend

- React SPA with server-side rendering for report pages.
- Dashboard built on a component library (e.g., shadcn/ui or Tremor) with chart library (Recharts or Nivo).
- Chat interface embedded in dashboard for Q&A agent.
- Mobile-responsive for alert review; full dashboard optimized for desktop.

### 7.6 Infrastructure

- Multi-tenant SaaS on AWS (or GCP).
- Containerized services (Docker + Kubernetes or ECS).
- CI/CD with automated testing.
- SOC 2 Type II as a medium-term target (important for fund and family office customers who will ask for it).

### Geopolitical Risk: Build vs. Buy

**GeoQuant** is the leading dedicated geopolitical risk data provider (40+ political risk indicators, 127 countries, hourly updates, PhD-vetted). However, pricing is enterprise-only and not publicly disclosed. Based on industry norms, expect $50K–$150K/year for institutional API access — too expensive to pass through to SMB customers at our price point.

**Recommended approach:**

For MVP and Starter/Pro tiers: Build a proprietary geo risk scoring layer using GDELT (free, 15-minute updates, 100+ languages) + ACLED (free for non-commercial/research; commercial license required for SaaS use, contact for pricing but significantly cheaper than GeoQuant) + LLM synthesis. Research shows GDELT+ACLED combined models achieve 87–94% AUC for conflict prediction — sufficient for a risk signal layer.

For Enterprise tier: Offer GeoQuant (or equivalent) as an optional data source that customers can connect via their own license, or negotiate a reseller arrangement. This keeps our cost base manageable while giving enterprise customers access to premium geo data if they need it.

ACLED commercial licensing should be the first step — it's the higher-quality conflict/event dataset and the pricing is likely accessible at our scale.

## 11. Key Risks & Open Questions

**Technical risks**
- LLM narrative quality: The narrative agent needs to be grounded in real data, not hallucinate. Requires strict tool-use discipline and output validation.
- Calculation precision: VaR and CVaR need to be actuarially defensible, not approximate. Recommend having a quant reviewer sign off on the calculation engine before customer-facing use.
- Data connector reliability: Third-party APIs go down or change. Need a robust failure mode that degrades gracefully (shows last-known values with freshness indicators) rather than breaking the dashboard.

**Business risks**
- Buying process at funds: Compliance officers and GPs at funds can be slow to adopt new tooling, especially for anything touching portfolio data. SOC 2 and data residency will be asked about early.
- "Good enough" competition: Excel + a quant analyst is the primary alternative. The value prop needs to be demonstrated, not just described — which means a compelling demo environment is critical.
- Segment focus: Funds and startups need genuinely different products under the hood. Starting with funds only (Segment A) may be the right call to avoid building two products at once.

**Open questions**
- Which custodian to integrate first? IB likely has the broadest coverage for the target fund segment.
- Build vs. buy for geopolitical risk scores? GeoQuant and similar providers are expensive. Could use LLM + GDELT + ACLED as a lower-cost alternative initially.
- How to handle private market valuations (PE, VC, real estate) for family offices? Resolved: manual upload (CSV/Excel or PDF) + LLM extraction to normalize holding data, then allocation is recorded in the position store. The Private Markets Analyst agent then applies ratings based on sector, geography, and vintage — using public comp data and macro signals as proxies where direct data is unavailable. Quarterly updates trigger a re-run of the Private Markets Analyst for affected holdings.
- Regulatory scope: Should the platform assist with Form PF, AIFMD, or other regulatory filings? This is a significant expansion but a strong wedge for fund customers.

# MVP Definition

## Guiding principle

The MVP's job is to prove one thing: an agent can build and maintain a useful risk dashboard with less effort than a human, for a paying customer who would not have had one otherwise.

Everything not needed to prove that should be cut.

---

## MVP Scope

### What's in

**One segment: Funds & Family Offices** (Segment A only)
Starting with two segments simultaneously would produce a mediocre product for both. Funds have clearer quantitative needs, higher willingness to pay, and a natural network for early sales.

**Three core risk modules:**
1. Portfolio VaR (historical simulation, 95% confidence, daily update)
2. Concentration risk (position, sector, geography)
3. Geopolitical exposure (powered by the Geopolitical Analyst agent — GDELT/ACLED + LLM synthesis, daily ratings per country)

**Three MVP analyst agents** (simplified scope of the full analyst bench):
1. Geopolitical Analyst — daily country risk ratings (1–5) for all countries represented in the portfolio, powered by GDELT + ACLED + LLM synthesis
2. Market Risk Analyst — daily market risk assessment covering equity volatility regime, cross-asset correlation, and macro stress signals (FRED + Polygon)
3. Credit Risk Analyst — daily credit risk assessment for fixed income holdings using public spread data and available rating agency signals

**Two data connectors:**
1. Interactive Brokers API (position data)
2. Polygon.io (market data for VaR calculation)
3. Manual CSV upload as fallback for any custodian without API access

**Dashboard:**

- Geopolitical exposure map

## MVP Build Sequence

### Phase 0 — Foundation (Weeks 1–3)
- Repo, CI/CD, infrastructure setup
- Data model: tenants, positions, metrics, narratives
- Auth (email + password; add SSO later)
- IB API connector (OAuth + position polling)
- Polygon.io connector (daily market data pull)
- CSV upload + normalization

### Phase 1 — Calculation Engine (Weeks 4–6)
- Historical simulation VaR engine (Python, numpy)
- Concentration metrics (HHI by sector, geography, single position)
- Rating store data model: analyst_rating {analyst_type, subject_id, subject_type, rating_date, score, confidence, outlook, drivers[], rationale_text, third_party_inputs[], model_version}
- Metric and rating storage, time-series history

### Phase 2 — Infrastructure Agent Layer (Weeks 7–9)
- Monitor agent: scheduled data pulls, freshness tracking
- Calculation agent: trigger recalculation on new data
- Narrative agent: LLM-generated explanation on metric change (grounded in data, tool-use pattern)
- Alert agent: threshold monitoring + email dispatch

### Phase 2b — MVP Analyst Agents (Weeks 8–10, parallel with Phase 2)
- Geopolitical Analyst: ISIN → country mapping, GDELT/ACLED ingestion, daily country risk ratings, LLM synthesis
- Market Risk Analyst: equity vol regime detection, cross-asset correlation signal, macro stress flags from FRED
- Credit Risk Analyst: credit spread monitoring for fixed income holdings, available rating inputs (free tier)
- Rating application layer: map portfolio holdings → relevant analyst ratings, weight by exposure
- Analyst divergence detection: flag when asset class and geography ratings conflict on the same holding

### Phase 3 — Dashboard & Onboarding (Weeks 10–12)
- Dashboard UI (summary card, VaR chart, concentration heatmap, geo map, narrative panel)
- Onboarding wizard (company profile → connectors → risk framework → first run)
- Limited Q&A chat (5 question types)
- Basic settings (thresholds, alert preferences)

### Phase 3b — Calibration Engine v1 (Weeks 11–13, parallel with Phase 3)
- Calibration data model: analyst_rating, calibration_event, signal_weight_log tables
- T+7 retrospective scheduler: every rating gets a review job queued 7 days out
- External calibration ingestion: listen for sovereign rating change announcements (public S&P/Fitch/Moody's), VIX regime shifts, and CDS proxy moves from already-connected market data
- Calibration agent: runs the T+7 post-mortem, scores directional accuracy and confidence, writes calibration_event records
- Retrieval-augmented memory for analyst agents: vector-index calibration records so agents can reference prior calibration outcomes in future reasoning
- Internal calibration dashboard (admin-only for now): Brier scores, early detection rate, false positive rate per analyst

### Phase 4 — Alpha (Weeks 14–16)
- 3–5 design partner customers onboarded
- Bug fixes, narrative quality tuning, data gap handling
- Instrumentation: track time-to-dashboard, data freshness, narrative ratings, calibration coverage
- First calibration cycle completes (T+7 reviews on earliest ratings issued during alpha)
- Analyst Track Record UI: basic view of rating accuracy for design partners to build trust

## MVP Tech Stack

| Layer | Choice | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Backend | Python (FastAPI) | Best ecosystem for quant calculations; async support |
| Calculation engine | Python (numpy, scipy, pandas) | Standard for financial calculations |
| Agent orchestration | Claude API with tool use (or LangGraph for multi-step) | Narrative + Q&A agents |
| Task queue | Celery + Redis | Async agent runs, scheduled pulls |
| Database | PostgreSQL + TimescaleDB extension | Relational + time-series in one system |
| File storage | S3 | Uploads, exports |
| Frontend | React + Tremor or shadcn/ui | Fast to build; good chart components |
| Charts | Recharts + react-simple-maps | VaR trends + geo map |
| Hosting | AWS (ECS or App Runner) | Straightforward, scales |
| Auth | Supabase Auth or Auth0 | Don't build this from scratch |
| Email | Resend or Postmark | Transactional alerts and digests |

## Definition of Done (MVP)

The MVP is complete when:
1. A fund PM can connect their IB account, get a VaR/concentration/geopolitical dashboard generated automatically, and receive an alert when a threshold is breached — without writing any code or formulas.
2. The agent-generated narrative for a VaR change is rated as accurate and useful by at least 4 out of 5 design partners.
3. Time from account creation to first populated dashboard is under 30 minutes for a user with an active IB account.
4. The system maintains data freshness (< 24-hour-old market data) for 95%+ of trading days without manual intervention.
5. The calibration engine has processed at least one full T+7 cycle across all three MVP analyst agents, and calibration records are being written correctly.
6. At least 2 design partners are willing to pay for continued access after the alpha period.
