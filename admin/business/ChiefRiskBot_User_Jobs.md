# ChiefRiskBot — User Jobs
*Jobs-to-be-Done framework · April 2026*

---

## How to read this

Each persona is mapped across four job types:

- **Functional jobs** — practical tasks they need to accomplish
- **Emotional jobs** — anxieties they want to resolve, feelings they want to have
- **Social jobs** — how they need to be perceived by others
- **Avoidance jobs** — outcomes they're actively trying to prevent

Then: current workarounds, trigger moments (when they'd reach for this product), and what a successful hire of ChiefRiskBot looks like.

---

## Persona A — The Professional Fund Manager

**Profile:** GP or PM at a fund with $50M–$500M AUM. Multi-asset (equities, fixed income, possibly alternatives). Accountable to LPs. Has quant literacy but no dedicated risk officer. Makes investment decisions daily while also managing LP relationships, compliance, and operations.

---

### Functional Jobs

**Monitor portfolio risk without building the infrastructure:**
"I want to know my portfolio's VaR, concentration, and geopolitical exposure right now — not after I've spent two hours pulling data from my OMS and running it through Excel."

**Detect regime changes before they hit the portfolio:**
"When cross-asset correlation spikes or a geopolitical event moves my EM exposure, I need to know before it shows up in my P&L."

**Explain risk to LPs in plain language:**
"When an LP asks why VaR went up 20% this quarter, I need a clear, defensible answer — not a number without context."

**Stay ahead of credit deterioration in fixed income holdings:**
"I want an early warning when the credit profile of a bond holding is worsening, not a surprise when a rating agency finally catches up."

**Generate a risk report without a team:**
"I need an LP-ready risk report quarterly. I don't have a risk officer to write it."

---

### Emotional Jobs

**Feel in control of tail risk:**
"The thing that keeps me up at night isn't volatility — it's the risk I haven't modeled. I want to feel like the major vectors are covered."

**Feel prepared before LP calls:**
"I never want to be caught off guard by a risk question in an LP meeting. I want to walk in having already seen what they're going to ask about."

**Reduce the cognitive overhead of risk monitoring:**
"I'm making investment decisions all day. I can't also be manually tracking 15 risk metrics. I want the monitoring to run itself."

---

### Social Jobs

**Be seen as a rigorous, institutional-quality manager:**
"I'm competing with larger funds for LP capital. A professional risk framework signals that I operate at their level, even without their headcount."

**Build LP confidence in the fund's risk discipline:**
"LPs are more sophisticated than they used to be. They want to see that risk isn't an afterthought — they're asking for it in diligence now."

---

### Avoidance Jobs

- Being blindsided by a drawdown they could have seen coming
- Losing an LP because they couldn't answer a risk question
- Realising their biggest risk is a concentration they didn't know they had
- Spending analyst time on risk reporting instead of research

---

### Current Workarounds

Excel models built once and barely maintained. Bloomberg PORT (if they have a terminal). Manual pulling from custodian portals. A quarterly PDF that takes two days to produce. Ad hoc Google searches when a geopolitical event moves the market.

---

### Trigger Moments

- A drawdown they couldn't explain
- An LP asking for a formal risk report for the first time
- A new LP in diligence asking about risk management process
- A geopolitical event that moved a position and they had no framework to interpret it
- Hiring a first analyst and wanting to give them a structured risk process

---

### What hiring ChiefRiskBot looks like

The fund manager opens the dashboard on Monday morning and sees: VaR is up 12% from last week, driven by a spike in EM correlation. The EM Asia Analyst flagged Taiwan Strait risk at 4/5 three days ago, which the Fixed Income Analyst confirmed with a widening on EM sovereign spreads. An alert already fired on Friday. The LP report for Q2 is one click away, auto-drafted with the narrative already written. The fund manager spends 20 minutes reviewing, adjusts one section, and sends it. None of this required a risk officer or a quant.

## Persona B — Head of a Family Office

**Profile:** CIO, COO, or CFO of a single or multi-family office managing $100M–$2B+ in total wealth. Preservation mandate — protecting and transferring wealth across generations, not maximising alpha. Portfolio spans public markets, private equity, real estate, direct lending, possibly art and collectibles. Manages multiple custodians. Accountable to the family patriarch, family council, or investment committee.

---

### Functional Jobs

**Get a consolidated view across all custodians and asset classes:**
"We have accounts at Schwab, Pershing, and a prime broker. We have PE allocations at six GPs, two real estate funds, and a direct lending book. I have no single view of what we actually own or how it all fits together."

**Monitor private market allocations without waiting for quarterly reports:**
"A PE fund I'm in just had a major portfolio company go through distress. I read about it in the FT. I should have known before that."

**Understand total geopolitical and concentration exposure:**
"We have significant EM exposure across public equities, a real estate fund in Southeast Asia, and a direct investment in a Turkish manufacturing company. I have no idea what the aggregate geopolitical risk picture looks like."

**Produce a risk summary for the investment committee:**
"The family council meets quarterly. They want a risk update. Right now that's a manual exercise every time."

**Track liquidity across the whole book:**
"If the family needed $50M in 90 days, I'd have to do a manual exercise to figure out what could be liquidated at what cost. That analysis doesn't exist in real time."

---

### Emotional Jobs

**Feel certain that nothing is being missed:**
"The nature of a family office is that there are always more things to monitor than bandwidth to monitor them. The anxiety isn't about any specific risk — it's about the risk I'm not seeing."

**Feel confident presenting to the family:**
"Family members range from a 72-year-old patriarch who doesn't want surprises to a 35-year-old grandchild who asks very specific questions. I need to be prepared for both."

**Feel in control of a complex, sprawling portfolio:**
"We've accumulated assets across 20 years and a dozen relationships. The complexity has grown faster than the tools to manage it."

---

### Social Jobs

**Demonstrate professional management to the family:**
"The family needs to see that their wealth is being managed with the same rigour as an institutional investor — not in spreadsheets."

**Maintain the trust of the investment committee:**
"The committee members are sophisticated. They can tell when a risk update is a real analysis versus a narrative assembled the night before."

**Justify the cost of the family office infrastructure:**
"Every year someone on the family council asks why we run an internal office instead of just using a multi-family office. A rigorous, tech-enabled risk process is part of the answer."

---

### Avoidance Jobs

- A correlated drawdown across public and private holdings that nobody modelled
- A family member finding out about a risk issue from the news before the family office flagged it
- An investment committee meeting where a question about risk exposure has no good answer
- Realising that two GPs have significant overlapping positions without knowing it
- A liquidity crunch when the family needs capital and illiquid positions dominate the book

---

### Current Workarounds

Separate custodian portals with no consolidation layer. Quarterly LP reports from PE managers that arrive weeks after quarter end and require manual aggregation. A spreadsheet maintained by one person that is always slightly out of date. Addepar or Orion for portfolio aggregation, but no risk layer on top. A consultant brought in once a year to run a risk review.

---

### Trigger Moments

- A market event that moved multiple parts of the portfolio in correlated ways, revealing unknown exposure
- A family council meeting where the risk question caught the CIO off guard
- A new investment committee member who asks for a formal risk framework
- A GP capital call that revealed how illiquid the book actually is
- Onboarding a new family member (next-generation) who wants transparency on the portfolio

---

### What hiring ChiefRiskBot looks like

The family office CIO arrives Monday. The dashboard shows a consolidated view across all custodians and the manually-uploaded PE and real estate allocations. The Private Markets Analyst has flagged that one PE fund's Southeast Asia exposure has elevated geopolitical risk (4/5, deteriorating) following last week's events in the region. The public equities in the same geography also show elevated risk — and the divergence panel notes that the Fixed Income Analyst still rates regional sovereign bonds as 2/5 (strong fundamentals). Before the quarterly investment committee meeting, the CIO generates a risk summary in one click. It already has the narratives written. The CIO reviews, adds a note on the PE fund, and circulates it. The committee meeting is the first one in three years where no risk question goes unanswered.

## Persona C — The Wealthy Individual

**Profile:** High-net-worth individual, $5M–$100M+ in investable assets. Not a professional investor — an entrepreneur, executive, or heir. Wealth is scattered: private equity fund allocations, a private banking relationship (UBS, JPMorgan, Julius Bär), possibly direct investments in a few companies, maybe real estate. Receives quarterly reports from each manager but reads few of them. Has no investment team, no CIO, and no consolidated view of anything.

---

### Functional Jobs

**Know what they actually own, in one place:**
"I have accounts at two private banks, three PE fund allocations, a direct stake in a friend's company, and two properties. I genuinely don't know my total net asset value at any given moment, let alone my risk profile."

**Understand without becoming an expert:**
"I don't want to learn VaR methodology. I want to know: is my money at risk right now, and where is the risk coming from — in plain English."

**Get early warning on something going wrong:**
"I don't want to find out a PE fund I'm in has a problem when I get the quarterly letter four months after it happened. I want to know when there's a signal."

**Prepare for conversations with their private bankers:**
"Every time I meet with my banker, I feel like I'm getting sold to rather than advised. I want to walk in knowing what questions to ask about my actual risk exposure."

**Understand how geopolitical events affect them personally:**
"When something happens in a market where I have exposure, I want to know immediately whether it matters to me — not have to call someone and wait."

---

### Emotional Jobs

**Feel informed, not dependent:**
"Right now I'm dependent on each manager telling me what they want me to know. I want an independent view."

**Reduce the anxiety of not knowing:**
"The main feeling is: I have a lot of money spread across a lot of things, and I don't know if something is quietly going wrong somewhere. That anxiety is low-grade but constant."

**Feel like a serious, sophisticated investor:**
"I've built wealth but I've never really managed it professionally. I want to feel like I have the same visibility as someone who has."

---

### Social Jobs

**Not be embarrassed in conversations with bankers or advisors:**
"I don't want to be the person who doesn't know their own portfolio. When a banker asks 'what are your biggest concerns about your current allocation?' I want to have an answer."

**Be able to brief family members or a spouse:**
"My partner occasionally asks how things are going. Right now the honest answer is 'I think fine but I'm not sure.' That's not a good answer."

---

### Avoidance Jobs

- A concentrated loss they could have reduced if they'd known about the risk
- Finding out about a problem from the news or a friend before their advisor told them
- Over-trusting a single bank's view because they had no independent perspective
- Illiquidity at a moment when they needed capital
- Paying advisory fees without knowing if the advice is actually managing their risk

---

### Current Workarounds

Reading (or not reading) quarterly PDFs from each manager. Occasional calls with their private banker. Asking a trusted friend or advisor informally. Aggregation via a private banking portal that shows balances but no risk view. An accountant who handles tax but not risk.

---

### Trigger Moments

- A market event that moved something they didn't know they had exposure to
- A private bank suggesting a new product they couldn't evaluate against their existing risk
- Hearing a friend discuss a risk that the user realised they had too
- An estate planning conversation that revealed how uncoordinated the portfolio is
- A divorce, inheritance, or liquidity event that suddenly required understanding the full picture
- Preparing to make a new investment and having no framework to assess it against existing positions

---

### What hiring ChiefRiskBot looks like

The user uploads their last quarterly statements from their private banks and PE managers. The LLM extraction pulls their positions and allocations, they confirm in a few clicks, and the dashboard is live. Three things are flagged: a PE fund has high EM Asia exposure that the Geopolitical Analyst rates 4/5; their Swiss private bank account has significant USD/CHF exposure they hadn't thought about as a risk; and two of their PE fund allocations are in the same sector, creating a concentration they didn't know existed. None of this required them to understand VaR. They read the narratives — written in plain language — and forward one of them to their banker before the next meeting. For the first time, they walk in with questions, not just answers to take.

## Persona D — The Private Banker / Wealth Manager

**Profile:** Relationship manager or senior advisor at a private bank (UBS, Julius Bär, Pictet, JPMorgan Private Bank) or a multi-family office (Bessemer, Pathstone, Threshold). Manages 30–80 client relationships, each with $5M–$200M+ in assets. Accountable to revenue targets (AUM, product placement, fee income). Technically sophisticated but not a quant. The client's trusted advisor — but also aware that clients have assets at other institutions they can't see.

---

### Functional Jobs

**Know what's happening across all of a client's wealth — including what they hold elsewhere:**
"My client has $40M with me, but I know they have another $60M at Credit Suisse and PE allocations with three GPs. When I give risk advice, I'm only seeing half the picture. I'm advising on a fragment."

**Demonstrate advisory value before the next client review:**
"Every quarterly meeting, I need to show the client something — an insight, a risk they hadn't considered, a reason this relationship is worth the fees. If I show up with nothing new, the relationship stagnates."

**Get ahead of events that will land in the client's inbox:**
"When the FT runs a story about a market the client has exposure to, I want to have already called them. Not be the third person they talk to."

**Identify concentration and cross-portfolio risks that client hasn't seen:**
"A client has EM equity exposure through their account with me, a Southeast Asia real estate fund through another manager, and a direct investment in a Turkish company. No one has mapped that aggregate geopolitical concentration. I want to be the one who does."

**Generate client-ready risk summaries without hours of manual work:**
"Producing a consolidated risk view for a client takes me half a day. I do it for my top 10 clients. The other 60 get nothing comparable. I need a way to scale it."

---

### Emotional Jobs

**Feel genuinely indispensable to the client:**
"The risk with private banking is that clients feel like they're being sold products, not advised. I want to be the person who calls them before they know they need to be called — the advisor who actually watches out for them."

**Feel confident in conversations that go beyond their own book:**
"When a client asks about risk in their PE portfolio that I don't manage, I usually deflect or give a generic answer. I want to be able to say something specific and credible."

**Feel differentiated from the next banker:**
"There are 15 private bankers competing for every HNW client. The ones who win long-term are the ones who provide visibility nobody else does. A consolidated risk view is exactly that."

---

### Social Jobs

**Be seen as the client's primary financial relationship, not one of several:**
"My goal is wallet consolidation over time. The more of a client's wealth picture I can see and advise on, the more natural it is for them to move assets to me."

**Be seen internally as a high-performer who uses technology to scale:**
"The bank is pushing digital advisory tools. Being an early adopter of a risk platform positions me well with management and justifies a higher-value client book."

---

### Avoidance Jobs

- Being the last person to warn a client about a risk that materialised
- Losing a client relationship to a competitor who offers better consolidated reporting
- A client finding out a risk through someone else, then asking why their banker didn't flag it
- Advising on risk when only seeing 40% of the client's total wealth
- Spending half a day building a risk summary that could be automated

---

### Current Workarounds

Risk reports produced manually for top clients. Generic market commentary sent to all clients regardless of their actual exposure. Conversations that deflect when asked about assets held elsewhere. Occasional use of the bank's internal portfolio tools, which only show in-house positions. No systematic early warning layer.

---

### Trigger Moments

- A client asks a risk question the banker can't answer about assets held elsewhere
- A competitor bank offers a client a "consolidated wealth view" as part of their pitch
- The bank launches a digital advisory initiative and the banker needs to show adoption
- A market event affects multiple clients simultaneously and the banker needs to respond quickly and personally at scale
- A major client relationship is at risk due to lack of advisory differentiation

---

### What hiring ChiefRiskBot looks like

The private banker onboards their top 20 clients — each connecting their external accounts or uploading quarterly statements. For each client, a consolidated risk view is live within the day. On Monday morning, the banker sees that two clients have elevated geopolitical exposure following last week's events: one through a direct investment, one through a PE allocation at another manager. The banker calls both before they read about it in the news. The client who moved $30M from a competitor six months ago calls back and says: "Nobody has ever called me about this before you." The banker's book grows.

## Cross-Persona Patterns

Three jobs appear across all three personas and should be treated as the core product promise:

1. **Consolidation** — all three have fragmented views of their own positions. The product's first job is to create one.

2. **Early warning** — all three are currently reactive. They find out about risk after it materialises. The product's second job is to move them from reactive to anticipatory.

3. **Explainability** — all three need to communicate risk to someone else (LPs, family council, private banker, spouse). The product's third job is to translate numbers into language they can use.

The analyst layer and calibration engine serve jobs 2 and 3. The connector framework and onboarding wizard serve job 1.
